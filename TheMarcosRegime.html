The Martial Law Era

The Darkest Age of the Philippines

Exactly at 7:17 PM on September 23, 1972, President Ferdinand Marcos appeared on National TV to announce he had declared Martial Law in the Philippines. He claimed that it was the last resort against the alleged communist insurgency threats of the Communist Party of the Philippines (CPP) and the movement of the Moro National Liberation Front (MNLF). Moreover, Marcos claimed that these groups planned to overthrow the government and stir up violence within the Filipino people. In reaction, Marcos claimed that he would proclaim martial law in the Philippines, as defined by the president's powers in the 1935 Philippine Constitution. Command over the Armed Forces of the Philippines to preserve law and order, as well as exclusive decision-making authority over whether or not a person would be imprisoned for any offense, were among the powers granted. The Armed Forces of the Philippines had absolute military control for 14 years.

The People Power Revolution

Call for Revolution

The People Power Revolution is a time of hope for many. It was known as a "bloodless revolution," as millions of Filipinos exhibited campaign against the violences caused by Marcos and the spirit of movement through non-violent demonstrations. This event is also referred to as the "Yellow Revolution" because of the presence of yellow ribbons as a reference to the song "Tie a Yellow Ribbon Round the Ole Oak Tree." Additionally, those present in the Revolution wore yellow as a homage to the assassination of Sen. Benigno "Ninoy" Aquino.
